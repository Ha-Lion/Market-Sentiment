How PublicSentimentDash Works

PublicSentimentDash is built to organize public financial narrative into a simple dashboard. It does not predict markets. It helps users understand what financial media and public market sources are emphasizing.

Step 1 — Source scanning

The engine scans public financial sources and collects market-related headlines across major categories such as indexes, bonds, forex, crypto, commodities, and macro themes.

  • Indexes and futures
  • Bonds and interest rates
  • Forex and currencies
  • Crypto assets
  • Commodities such as gold, oil, silver, copper, and natural gas
  • Macro themes such as the Fed, inflation, jobs, GDP, tariffs, and geopolitics

Step 2 — Headline relevance classification

Each headline is checked for market categories, instruments, and high-impact keywords. The goal is to separate useful market headlines from generic noise.

Directly Relevant

A headline that clearly mentions a specific market, instrument, asset, rate, currency, or macro driver.

Indirectly Relevant

A headline that points to a broader market category, theme, or macro condition without a precise instrument.

Irrelevant

A headline that does not carry enough financial-market relevance for the dashboard.

Step 3 — Sentiment vote

The dashboard estimates whether a headline or source appears bullish, bearish, mixed, or neutral based on public wording and market context.

This is not the same as a trade recommendation. Sentiment shows tone and narrative pressure, not certainty.

Step 4 — Market focus

The system counts which markets and instruments are mentioned most often in the latest scan. This helps users see where public financial attention is concentrated.

Step 5 — Technical direction

For supported instruments, the dashboard also calculates a simple technical direction using price-based checks.

  • SMA20
  • SMA50
  • Close vs SMA20
  • Close vs SMA50
  • SMA20 vs SMA50 trend
  • 10-candle slope
  • RSI14

How users should read the dashboard

The dashboard is most useful as a fast market-awareness layer. Stronger confidence usually comes when public sentiment, technical direction, and fundamental conditions all point in the same direction.

When those layers conflict, the market picture is less clean and should be treated with caution.
